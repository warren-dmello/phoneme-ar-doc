Augmented Reality for Phonological Learning in Neurodiverse Children

An ongoing research project by Warren D'Mello

Goa, India 2024–Present ★ IEEE Best Paper Award KMD Application 2026

PhonemeAR is a browser-based Tangible-Augmented Reality system that lets neurodiverse children build words by scanning physical phoneme cards with a smartphone camera. Rather than remediating deficits through repetition, the system activates spatial, tactile, and visual-kinesthetic capabilities that screen-based phonics tools do not reach. A teacher mediates every session, choosing which phonemes to introduce, at what pace, and in what sequence, while an accompanying Learner Strength Profile instrument generates a personalised radar chart that configures session parameters around each child's cognitive profile. Developed independently as a single-file HTML application requiring no installation, PhonemeAR is currently in the pilot study phase at Anand Niketan Special School in Mapusa, Goa.

The Problem

What existing tools get wrong

Most phonics software for neurodiverse children is designed around remediation: it identifies what a child struggles with and delivers more of it. The underlying assumption (that difficulty is the primary signal worth responding to) produces tools that are, by design, deficit-facing. A child's struggles become the interface. This is not a minor pedagogical choice; it is a structural one, and it shapes everything from interaction design to session pacing.

Phonological processing is a specific and frequently under-addressed gap in this picture. Phonological awareness, meaning the ability to recognise and manipulate the sound structure of words independent of their spelling, is the cognitive foundation for early reading. It is also the area where neurodiverse children, particularly those with dyslexia and related language processing differences, most often fall behind. Yet the dominant digital response to this gap is screen-based drill: abstract, unembodied, and entirely dependent on the visual-linguistic channel that these children find most taxing.

Augmented reality, in this context, is not a gimmick. It is a specific response to a specific failure mode. Many neurodiverse children have strong spatial, tactile, and visual-kinesthetic capabilities that conventional interfaces simply do not engage. Physical phoneme cards as AR triggers make the act of building words tangible and visible: the child's hands are doing the phonological work, not their eyes alone. The tool is also constrained by design: it requires a facilitating teacher, it works within the existing classroom relationship, and it does not attempt to replace pedagogical judgment. Every architectural decision follows from that constraint.

Research Foundation

Theoretical grounding

PhonemeAR is grounded in five bodies of work that converge on a single design philosophy: start from what a learner can do, not what they cannot. Ishii and Ullmer's Tangible Bits framework (1997) provides the foundational argument for coupling physical objects to digital information. The phoneme cards are not controllers; they are the primary cognitive artefacts. Antle's work on embodied child-computer interaction (2009) establishes why tactile card manipulation, rather than touchscreen gesture, is the right primary input modality for this age group and cognitive profile. Ishido's research on tactile media and neurodiversity (2024) directly informs the multi-sensory feedback design. Snowling's phonological deficit hypothesis (2000) justifies the phoneme-level granularity of the word-building task. And Armstrong's documentation of spatial and narrative strengths in dyslexia (2010) is the direct source of the strength-based design philosophy: the conviction that a learning system should discover and amplify human strengths rather than map and measure deficits.

The Learner Strength Profile instrument extends this grounding through Goswami's research on auditory-rhythmic processing (2011), Vygotsky's zone of proximal development for social-relational session design, and trauma-informed practice literature for the frustration response dimension.

The System

PhonemeAR: how it works

PhonemeAR is a single-file HTML application. There is no build step, no backend, and no installation required. A teacher opens the URL on any smartphone, selects 2–8 phonemes from the core working set, and the application generates unique card images and compiles AR tracking data entirely in-browser, a process that takes 10–30 seconds on mobile, managed with a progress bar. The teacher downloads the cards as PNG files to print, or displays them full-screen on a laptop. The child then points the phone camera at the physical cards and words form in augmented reality as cards are combined.

Each valid phoneme combination triggers the Web Speech API to read the sequence aloud, spell it, and say the whole word again, forming a three-pass auditory reinforcement loop. After each session, the teacher sees a summary screen showing words built, accuracy percentage, and average attempt time. A JSON log is available for export, but only after the teacher has reviewed the human-readable summary. This gates raw data behind a pedagogical review step by design.

The decision to build as a single-file browser app was a deployment constraint, not a technical preference. The target environment is a special school in Goa where teacher technical literacy varies and app installation introduces friction. A shareable URL that works on any smartphone removes every barrier between the teacher and the tool. Updates deploy instantly without any action required from the school.

The current working phoneme set is nine phonemes: k, a, t, m, s, ee, p, n, i. Cards use the phoneme symbol rather than the letter: k represents the /k/ sound regardless of spelling. This is deliberate: the tool targets phonological awareness (sound recognition and manipulation), which is a distinct cognitive skill from spelling instruction. The set generates 24 valid CVC and CV words across three vowel patterns.

Learner Strength Profile

Configuring the system around the learner

The Learner Strength Profile (LSP) is a companion assessment instrument that runs a 19-question structured observation across seven dimensions of learner profile. It is designed to be completed by the facilitating teacher, not the child, and takes approximately five minutes. The output is a radar chart and a recommended phoneme set and session configuration for the AR Builder, covering which phonemes to introduce, how long hints should last, and whether the session should be solo or facilitated. The relationship between the two tools is bidirectional: the Strength Mapper configures the AR session, and the AR session's logs update the profile over time as real interaction data accumulates. The system learns from how the child actually behaves, not just from what a pre-session assessment captures.

Pilot Study

Testing in the field

Currently in progress at Anand Niketan Special School, Mapusa, Goa

The system has been validated technically. The pilot study is designed to validate educational impact, surfacing the things that only field deployment reveals. Sessions are conducted at Anand Niketan Special School in Mapusa, Goa, with neurodiverse children aged 5–8. Mr. Babi Malik has confirmed agreement to facilitate the pilot. Parental consent forms and child assent are required before any session begins.

The pilot is structured around four research questions. First: does the child engage with the physical cards as the primary interaction object, or does the AR overlay become the dominant attractor? Second: does the AR component add to the phoneme-building task, or does it distract from it? Third: what error patterns appear in the session logs, specifically the ratio of wrong-order errors (correct phonemes, wrong arrangement) to wrong-phoneme errors? Fourth: can the facilitating teacher operate the tool without developer support? The last question is treated as a binary usability constraint: if the teacher needs help, the tool has failed on its own terms.

Session structure follows a consistent arc: teacher introduction, phoneme selection, in-browser card compilation (10–30 seconds), a 10–15 minute scanning session, and review of the session summary screen. JSON session logs are exported for analysis. That the pilot is ongoing rather than complete is not a limitation. It is the honest state of empirically grounded educational technology research, where the most important validation is always the one that happens in the room with the children.

The Researcher

Warren D'Mello

Photo

Warren D'Mello is a Computer Engineering graduate from Goa University (2025) working at the intersection of human-computer interaction, tangible interaction, and inclusive education. PhonemeAR emerged from fieldwork at special education schools in Goa and a conviction that learning systems should discover and amplify human strengths rather than measure deficits. The project sits at a specific intersection of AR engineering, phonological theory, and strength-based pedagogy, one that required building the research instrument alongside the intervention itself. He is currently applying to graduate programmes in HCI and media design, including the Keio University Graduate School of Media Design, to continue this work in a structured academic environment with access to the interdisciplinary methods it requires.
